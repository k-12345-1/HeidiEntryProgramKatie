Anti-CYA Design

How this system structurally prevents defensive documentation

CYA documentation happens when clinicians write for lawyers, not patients. The shared layer must make this structurally impossible — not just discouraged.
1
No free text in the shared layer
The shared summary contains only structured, discrete data. No free-text field exists. A practitioner who wants to say "patient is non-compliant" simply cannot — the field doesn't exist.
2
Flags replace opinions
Cross-discipline communication uses structured flags with a domain and directionality. The template constrains language — a flag cannot contain "I documented this in case..." — it can only describe a clinical fact.
3
Authorship is visible and permanent
Every item shows who flagged it and when. Practitioners cannot anonymously add liability-shifting statements. Permanence and attribution make CYA notes a reputational risk rather than a safe harbour.
4
The summary is synthesised, not aggregated
Capsule synthesises — pulling structured data into a unified model. A GP cannot see the physio's reasoning or the psychologist's uncertainty. They see clinical facts. This prevents knowledge of another clinician's documentation from influencing one's own notes.
5
Psychological safety for honest clinical communication
Psychologists can share a clinical flag without exposing session content that might be misread out of context. The system reduces the cost of honest communication — CYA notes are often a response to feeling exposed.
6
Conflicts are system-detected, not practitioner-reported
Conflicts surface based on clinical logic — opposing directionality on a shared domain — not interpersonal dynamics. Neither practitioner has to report the other. Practitioners are not incentivised to document the failure of others.
